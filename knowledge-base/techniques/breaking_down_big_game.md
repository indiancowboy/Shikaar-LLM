# Breaking Down Big Game

Whether it's a whitetail, an elk, a bear, or a hog, the carcass breaks down into the same handful of primals. Knowing what each one is good for is the difference between a freezer full of mystery packages and a freezer you actually cook out of.

## The premium cuts — cook hot and fast
- **Backstrap (loin):** The two long muscles running along the spine. The most tender meat on the animal. Cut into medallions or leave whole and sear hot — never overcook. Treat it like a ribeye. On a bear or hog it's a little fattier and forgiving.
- **Tenderloins (inner loins):** Tucked inside the body cavity along the spine. Small, buttery, even more tender than backstrap. Pull these first when field dressing so they don't dry out. Perfect for a quick high-heat sear or kebabs.

## The hindquarter — roasts, steaks, and jerky
The back leg is the biggest chunk of meat on the animal. It separates into individual muscle groups (top round, bottom round, sirloin tip, eye of round). Options:
- Keep large muscles whole as **roasts** (great braised or for tandoori-style spiced roasts).
- Slice across the grain into **steaks** or thin **cutlets**.
- The bottom round and eye are leaner and tougher — ideal for **jerky** or grinding.

## The front shoulder — low and slow
The front leg and shoulder (chuck) is worked muscle full of connective tissue. It does not want to be a steak. It wants time and moisture:
- **Shoulder roasts** for braises, curries, and pulled-game (wild hog shoulder → vindaloo or Goan pulled pork is a classic).
- Bone-in or boneless, it shreds beautifully after a few hours.

## Shanks, neck, and ribs — the slow-cook gold
- **Shanks:** The lower legs. All collagen. Braise them whole (osso buco style) until they fall apart — some of the best eating on the animal, and most people grind them by mistake.
- **Neck:** Tough but flavorful — braise or grind.
- **Ribs:** Worth it on a fat bear or hog; on a lean deer most people strip the meat for grind.

## Trim and grind
Everything left — flank, rib meat, odd scraps — becomes **ground**. Lean game (deer, elk, antelope) has almost no fat, so plan to add fat when you grind (see field-to-freezer notes). Ground game is the workhorse: keema, kofta, burgers, sausage.

## Rough yield expectation
Edible boneless meat runs roughly 40–50% of an animal's live weight for lean big game, and less for a fatty bear once you trim it down. Use that to sanity-check what should come back from the processor — a 300 lb bear yielding 200 lbs of "meat" means they didn't trim the fat.
