# Handling Bear & Fatty Game

Bear, wild hog, and waterfowl are a different animal from lean deer — literally. They carry real fat, and that fat changes how you butcher, cook, and store them.

## Cook it to temperature — this one is non-negotiable
Bear and wild hog can carry **Trichinella** (and hogs can carry other pathogens). There is no "medium-rare bear." Cook bear and wild hog to an internal **160°F (71°C)** throughout. This is the single most important rule with these animals — freezing does **not** reliably kill trichinella in bear, so temperature is your safeguard. Lean venison you can cook to a rosy medium; bear and hog you cannot.

## Bear fat is worth saving
Unlike lean deer, bear carries a thick fat cap. Rather than tossing it:
- **Render it.** Trim the fat, cut it small, and render low and slow into clean white bear fat (essentially lard). Excellent for frying, pastry, and confit. A fall bear on acorns and berries renders sweeter fat than a spring bear.
- Trim hard fat off cuts you're freezing as steaks/roasts — it's the fat that goes rancid first and drives the short shelf life.

## Why fatty game spoils faster in the freezer
Fat oxidizes and goes rancid far quicker than lean muscle, even frozen. That's why the shelf-life math treats fat content as the main driver:
- **Bear and wild hog:** about 6 months vacuum-sealed, 4 months wrapped.
- **Waterfowl (duck, goose, teal):** same ballpark — 6 months vac, 4 wrapped.
- Compare lean **big game** at up to 24 months vac-sealed.

The fix is simple: trim excess hard fat before freezing, vacuum-seal rather than paper-wrap, and cook the fatty animals first.

## Grinding fatty game
Hog and bear grind well on their own — they bring their own fat, unlike deer. For hog sausage, the existing fat usually carries it; for a leaner bear you can still add a little pork fat for sausage but you rarely need much. Keep ground bear/hog cold and cook it promptly to temperature.
