# Field to Freezer

Great wild-game cooking is won or lost before the meat ever hits the pan. How you cool, age, trim, and store it matters as much as any recipe.

## Cool it fast
The clock starts the moment the animal is down. Get the hide off and the carcass cooling quickly — heat is what spoils meat and turns on that "gamey" flavor people complain about. In warm weather, get it on ice or into a cooler. Keep the meat clean and dry.

## Aging (optional, but it helps)
If you can hang the carcass at just-above-freezing (34–40°F) for 5–10 days, aging tenderizes the meat and mellows the flavor. No cold space? Skip it — a fresh-processed deer is still great. Don't "age" in a warm garage; that's just spoilage.

## Trim hard before you freeze
- Remove **silver skin** (the shiny connective membrane) from backstraps and roasts — it won't break down and turns rubbery.
- Trim off hard fat and any blood-shot or hair-covered meat. On lean deer the fat is waxy and tallowy and goes off fast — get it off.

## Grinding lean game — add fat
Deer, elk, and antelope are so lean that straight ground meat cooks up dry and crumbly. When you grind, add fat:
- **~10–20% pork fat or beef tallow** for burgers and general grind.
- **~20–30%** for sausage, where you want richness and bind.
- Bear and hog usually need little or none — they bring their own.

Keep everything ice-cold while grinding so the fat smears clean instead of mushing.

## Packaging for the long haul
- **Vacuum-seal** whenever you can. Removing air is the single biggest factor in preventing freezer burn and extending shelf life — it roughly doubles the life versus paper.
- **Butcher/freezer paper** works for short-term storage but lets in air; wrapped cuts have noticeably shorter shelf life.
- Freeze in **meal-sized portions** and label every package with species, cut, and date. The date is what lets you cook the oldest stuff first.

## First in, first out
Shelf life depends on the animal's fat content: lean big game lasts up to ~24 months vacuum-sealed, fatty bear/hog and waterfowl closer to 4–6 months, and oily fish degrade fastest of all. Cook by date, lead with whatever's aging out, and trust your nose over any chart.
