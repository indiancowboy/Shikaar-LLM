# Cambodian-Style Deer Tenderloin

## Description
In Siem Reap, I savored Cambodian roast chicken marinated in soy and fish sauce, garlic, fire-roasted, and glazed with oyster sauce. It was charred to perfection and enjoyed with delectable pepper sauce. These sweet, salty, funky, umami bomb flavors inspired this recipe. It’s an interesting way to showcase the flavors of Cambodia combined with some modern techniques.
For this recipe, I would recommend following the sous vide method, but you can prepare it in the oven or over a grill just fine. I also recommend buying Kampot pepper. It may be pricey, but it is by far the best pepper in the world. 



Ingredients
Venison backstrap or tenderloin 
Compound Butter: 
1 cup (2 sticks) butter, softened 
3 tablespoons garlic cloves 
2 tablespoons soy sauce 
2 tablespoons fish sauce 
2 tablespoons freshly cracked Kampot pepper 
Tuk Meric (pepper sauce): 
1/4 cup Kampot pepper 
1/4 cup freshly squeezed lime juice (from 3 limes)
2 teaspoons sugar 
2 teaspoons salt 
Glaze: 
3 tablespoons oyster sauce 
2 tablespoons soy sauce 
2 tablespoons rice wine vinegar 
5 teaspoons minced garlic
1/2 tablespoon sugar 
Preparation
Preheat your sous vide bath to 120°F (see note). In a medium bowl, mix together the butter, garlic, soy sauce, fish sauce, and pepper until combined. Spread it all over your backstrap/tenderloin. Transfer the tenderloin to a ziplock or vacuum-seal bag and seal. Add the bag to the water bath and cook, undisturbed, for 3 hours. 

Meanwhile, make the Tuk Meric (Cambodian pepper sauce). Toast the Kampot peppercorns until they become fragrant. Transfer to a heatproof mortar and pestle and roughly crush. In a small bowl, combine the salt, sugar, and lime juice. Whisk until no particles remain. Stir in the pepper and set aside.

Next, make the glaze. In a medium bowl, whisk together the oyster sauce, soy sauce,  seasoned rice wine vinegar, garlic, and sugar. 

Remove the bag from the water bath using tongs. Open the bag and lather the venison with the glaze. Finish the backstrap over hot coals, constantly brushing the glaze. Once the venison reaches an internal temperature of 130-135°F, it’s done! Slice and  serve on a platter or large plate with the Tuk Meric in a bowl in the middle.
